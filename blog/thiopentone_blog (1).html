The Chemistry

What makes it what it is · The sulfur story

Thiopental is derived from barbituric acid. The defining feature is sulfur at position 2 — replacing the oxygen found in oxybarbiturates like pentobarbital. This single substitution changes everything. Sulfur dramatically increases lipid solubility. And lipid solubility is everything in anaesthesia.

More lipid soluble means faster crossing of the blood-brain barrier. Faster brain penetration. Faster onset. This is why thiobarbiturates dominate as induction agents.

Structural Modification Example Effect
Oxygen at position 2 Pentobarbital, Secobarbital Less lipid soluble — oxybarbiturates
Sulfur at position 2 Thiopental, Thiamylal MORE lipid soluble · GREATER hypnotic potency
Phenyl at position 5 Phenobarbital Increases anticonvulsant activity
Methyl on nitrogen Methohexital Increases hypnotic potency · Lowers seizure threshold
One atom of sulfur. One decision by nature. And anaesthesia was never the same again.

The Mechanism

How it silences the brain · GABAA and the chloride channel

Thiopental works on the GABAA receptor — the primary inhibitory receptor in the central nervous system. It does not activate it directly at clinical doses. It potentiates it. It makes GABA — the brain's own braking system — work harder, longer, more effectively.

Primary Mechanism — Three Levels
→Allosteric Modulation: Increases GABA affinity for GABAA receptors at clinical doses
→Prolonged Opening: Extends the duration of Cl⁻ channel opening — more inhibition per receptor activation
→Direct Activation: At HIGH doses, directly activates GABAA receptors — bypasses the need for GABA entirely

The specific subunits that matter are the β3 subunits of GABAA receptors — these mediate the immobilising and hypnotic activities. Additionally, thiopental has ancillary actions on glutamate, adenosine, and neuronal nicotinic receptors — but GABAA is the star of the story.

💡 Exam Pearl

Clinical doses = GABA potentiation via allosteric binding. High doses = direct GABAA activation. This explains the dose-dependent progression: sedation → hypnosis → anaesthesia → profound CNS depression in overdose.

The Redistribution Story

Why patients wake up · The most testable concept in IV anaesthesia

Here is the concept that trips every student who hasn't sat with it long enough: thiopental has a long elimination half-life. And yet patients wake up in minutes. How? The answer is not in the liver. It is in the physics of lipid solubility and tissue distribution.

Thiopental does not leave the body quickly. It simply moves.

Phase 1 — Rapid Brain Uptake → Unconsciousness in Seconds

Highly lipophilic thiopental crosses the blood-brain barrier almost instantaneously. The brain receives a surge of drug concentration. Consciousness dissolves within one arm-brain circulation time. This is the induction.

Phase 2 — Redistribution → Awakening in 5–10 Minutes

Now the drug follows its lipophilicity gradient. Skeletal muscle — highly perfused and large in mass — draws the drug away from the brain. Brain concentration falls. Consciousness returns. The drug has not been eliminated — it has simply moved. This redistribution is why the single-dose duration is short.

Phase 3 — Slow Hepatic Metabolism

99% of thiopental is eventually metabolised in the liver to inactive products (hydroxythiopental, 5-carboxylic acid). But this process is slow — clinically irrelevant for a single dose, since the patient is already awake from redistribution long before metabolism completes.

The brain is a tide pool.
Redistribution is the ocean drawing back.
The drug is still there — just no longer where it matters.
⚠️
After prolonged infusion, drug accumulates in fat and muscle and slowly re-enters the circulation — the context-sensitive half-time becomes very long. This is why thiopental is unsuitable for maintenance infusions — and why propofol, with its rapid hepatic clearance, replaced it for TIVA.

Potency & Dose

Ionisation · Lipid solubility · Why cardiac output is everything

Thiopental at physiological pH 7.4 is 61% nonionised — and it is the nonionised fraction that crosses the BBB. The more nonionised the drug, the greater its brain penetration, and therefore its potency.

Drug Relative Potency Nonionised at pH 7.4
Thiopental 1.0 (reference) 61%
Thiamylal 1.1 Similar
Methohexital 2.5 (most potent) 76%
💡 Exam Pearl

Methohexital is 2.5× more potent because 76% is nonionised vs thiopental's 61%. Greater nonionised fraction = easier BBB crossing = greater potency. This is the ionisation principle applied in practice.

Now — perhaps the most clinically critical concept in thiopental dosing. We modify the dose based on several factors. But one towers above all others:

Decrease Dose
Elderly · Pregnancy (7–13 weeks) · Hypovolemia · Low cardiac output
Increase Dose
Thermal injury — children more than 1 year post-burn
No Change
Alcoholism — after 9–30 days of abstinence
Why Cardiac Output Is the Most Important Factor

Low cardiac output means less drug is delivered to skeletal muscle and fat. The drug stays in circulation. More reaches the brain. A standard induction dose in a patient with low CO — shock, heart failure, severe hypovolaemia — can produce profound and prolonged CNS depression and cardiovascular collapse. Always reduce the dose. Always.

The Neuro Applications

ICP · Cerebral protection · The burst suppression target

This is where thiopental becomes not just pharmacologically interesting, but clinically consequential. In neurosurgery, in the ICU, in the trauma bay — the ability of a drug to reduce intracranial pressure and protect the brain is not a footnote. It is everything.

The ICP Reduction Cascade
→Thiopental reduces cerebral metabolic rate of O₂ (CMRO₂)
→Fewer vasodilatory metabolites produced
→Cerebral vasoconstriction
→↓ Cerebral blood volume → ↓ Cerebral blood flow
→↓ ICP

The target in refractory ICP is EEG burst suppression. At isoelectric EEG — maximum CMRO₂ depression — approximately 55% of cerebral metabolic activity is suppressed. This is the ceiling of what thiopental can achieve.

⚠️
Thiopental for ICP reduction comes with a price: significant hypotension from peripheral vasodilation. And despite theoretical neuroprotection, no demonstrated improvement in outcome has been shown in head trauma patients. The haemodynamic cost may negate the cerebral benefit in the wrong patient.

The Systemic Effects

Cardiovascular · Respiratory · What matters at the bedside

Thiopental is not a cardiotoxin. It is not a myocardial depressant — not at clinical doses. The hypotension it produces is through a different and teachable mechanism.

Cardiovascular — The True Mechanism of Hypotension

Primary mechanism: Peripheral vasodilation via depression of the medullary vasomotor centre → reduced sympathetic outflow. In normovolaemic patients — BP falls transiently 10–20 mmHg, HR rises compensatorily 15–20 bpm. Effects are mild and transient.

Clinical implication: In hypovolaemia or cardiovascular compromise — these "mild" changes become dramatic. The vasodilatation in a patient who is already vasodilated is catastrophic. This is why dose reduction in shock is not optional.

Respiratory — Prepare for Apnoea

Thiopental produces dose-dependent ventilatory depression by acting on the medullary and pontine respiratory centres. CO₂ sensitivity decreases. After a standard induction dose — respiratory rate slows, tidal volume decreases.

Apnoea is especially likely when thiopental is combined with other CNS depressants. Laryngeal reflexes are preserved at clinical doses — but do not rely on this in an inadequately anaesthetised patient. Have your airway equipment ready. Always.

The Dangers

Complications that demand respect · Errors that must never happen

⚠️
Intra-Arterial Injection — An Emergency

Thiopental at 2.5% is highly alkaline. Intra-arterial injection causes immediate intense vasoconstriction, excruciating pain along the artery's distribution, blanching followed by cyanosis, and obliteration of distal pulses. The risk is gangrene and permanent nerve damage.

Emergency Management: Do NOT remove the needle. Immediately dilute with saline through the same needle. Inject vasodilators — lignocaine or papaverine — through the line. Maintain adequate blood flow. This is a limb-threatening emergency.
⚠️
Allergic Reactions — Rare but Severe. True anaphylaxis or anaphylactoid reaction — incidence approximately 1 in 30,000. Can occur without prior exposure. History of atopy is a risk factor. Management: stop administration, epinephrine, IV fluids, antihistamines, supportive care.
Enzyme Induction — The Hidden Interaction

Sustained administration (2–7 days) induces hepatic enzymes. This accelerates metabolism of oral anticoagulants, phenytoin, tricyclics, corticosteroids, bile salts, and Vitamin K.

Critical warning: Thiopental accelerates heme production and may exacerbate acute intermittent porphyria. It is contraindicated in known porphyria.

The Cousin

Methohexital · Where the rules are different

Methohexital is a barbiturate in the same family — but with a single, critical difference that inverts everything you know about barbiturates and seizures.

The Paradox of Methohexital
→All other barbiturates raise the seizure threshold → anticonvulsant
→Methohexital lowers the seizure threshold → proconvulsant
→This makes it uniquely useful for temporal lobe seizure focus identification during epilepsy surgery
→And for ECT — where longer seizure duration is clinically desired

The price of this property: high incidence of excitatory phenomena — myoclonus, hiccoughs, involuntary movements. These are dose-dependent and can be reduced with opioid pretreatment.

The ten things you must never forget

And now — let me give you the ten truths of thiopental.

Not as bullet points. As principles.

Because pharmacology taught as bullet points is pharmacology forgotten by morning. These ten concepts are the skeleton of everything we have discussed — and they will appear on your exams in one form or another, because they represent the fundamental pharmacology of all IV anaesthetics, not just thiopental.

  1. 1Redistribution, not metabolism, determines the duration of a single dose. The brain wakes up because the drug moves — not because the liver removes it.
  2. 2The pathway is always Brain → Muscle → Fat. In that sequence. Because of perfusion and mass.
  3. 3The single most important dose-modifying factor is cardiac output. Low CO = more drug stays in circulation = higher brain concentration = less drug needed.
  4. 4Never use for infusions. The context-sensitive half-time is prohibitively long. Drug accumulates in fat and haunts the patient.
  5. 5ICP reduction mechanism: CMRO₂ suppression → vasoconstriction → ↓CBV → ↓ICP.
  6. 6Metabolism is 99% complete and entirely hepatic — but for a single dose, it is clinically irrelevant.
  7. 7Hypotension comes from peripheral vasodilation — depression of the medullary vasomotor centre. Not from direct myocardial depression.
  8. 8Respiratory: always prepare for apnoea. Have airway equipment ready before you push the drug.
  9. 9Intra-arterial injection: leave the needle in, dilute immediately, vasodilate. Gangrene is the alternative.
  10. 10Thiopental has been replaced by propofol — but it remains the prototype by which all IV anaesthetics are understood.
