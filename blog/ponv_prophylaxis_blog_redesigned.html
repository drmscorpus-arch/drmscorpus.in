Why PONV Still Matters

The scale of the problem · Why we cannot ignore it

Without prophylaxis, nausea and vomiting affects roughly 30% of all surgical patients. In high-risk patients — women undergoing laparoscopic procedures, patients with a prior history, those needing postoperative opioids — this number climbs to 80%.

That is not a side effect. That is a crisis happening in the recovery room every single day.

The consequences extend beyond discomfort. Delayed PACU discharge. Unplanned admissions from day-surgery. Dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, wound dehiscence. And at the extreme end — aspiration and oesophageal rupture. But even without the complications, there is a quieter harm: the patient who feared surgery to begin with, and now fears it more.

"Patients are willing to pay out-of-pocket to prevent PONV. They rank it above postoperative pain. That should tell us everything we need to know about how seriously we must take it."

The Apfel Score

Four questions · One number · Everything changes

Before we talk about prophylaxis, we must talk about risk. Because the evidence is clear — we should not give every patient the same prophylaxis. We risk-stratify. And for that, we have the Apfel Score.

Simple. Four factors. Each one adds one point. Each point adds approximately 20% to a baseline risk of 10%.

The Four Apfel Risk Factors

Each factor adds 1 point · Each point adds ~20% to baseline risk of 10%

  1. Female gender — the strongest single predictor. Odds Ratio of 3.
  2. Non-smoking status — smokers, paradoxically, have lower PONV rates. OR of 2.
  3. History of PONV or motion sickness — the patient who vomited last time will likely vomit again. OR of 2.
  4. Postoperative opioid use — the price of pain control, when it is opioid-based. OR of 2.
Apfel Score PONV Risk Category
0 factors 10% Low
1 factor 20% Low
2 factors 40% Moderate
3 factors 60% High
4 factors 80% Very High

And here is something worth pausing on — why does smoking protect against PONV? The most common theory: polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons in cigarette smoke induce cytochrome P450 enzymes, increasing metabolism of emetogenic volatile anaesthetics. The evidence supporting this is limited. But the association is real and reproducible.

Factors that do NOT increase PONV risk — the disproved list
✗Body mass index (BMI)
✗Menstrual cycle phase
✗Remifentanil vs fentanyl — short-acting opioids offer no advantage
✗Supplemental oxygen at FiO₂ 80% — does not help
✗Nasogastric tube use

The Drug Arsenal

What the Cochrane review showed · Ranked by efficacy

The 2020 Cochrane Network Meta-Analysis is the most important piece of evidence we have on PONV prophylaxis. 585 randomised controlled trials. 97,500 patients. 44 single agents. 51 drug combinations. It is the most comprehensive analysis ever conducted — and it changed how we think about antiemetics forever.

The Cochrane review changed everything. It showed us that NK1 antagonists are not just better — they are in a different league entirely.

And the most remarkable finding of all — the finding that changes clinical practice — is this:

Every other antiemetic works better in combination than alone.
NK1 antagonists work as well alone as most combinations of other drugs.
That is unique. That has never been shown for any other class of antiemetic.

The NK1 Antagonists

Aprepitant · Fosaprepitant · Why they are different

NK1 receptor antagonists target substance P receptors in the nucleus tractus solitarius — the vomiting centre. This is a completely different mechanism from the 5-HT₃ antagonists and dopamine antagonists that we have relied on for decades. Originally developed for chemotherapy-induced nausea, they have transformed our approach to the perioperative setting.

The Two NK1 Antagonists

Aprepitant (Emend®)
80mg oral
1–3 hours pre-op
Duration: 48–72 hours
→
Fosaprepitant
150mg IV
Pre-op or intraop

Both offer 48–72 hour protection. No sedation. No QT prolongation. No extrapyramidal effects. No delay in recovery.

The 2025 systematic review confirmed what the Cochrane analysis had suggested — the 80mg dose of aprepitant is clearly superior to the 40mg dose. Not just for vomiting, but for nausea, rescue antiemetic use, and complete response. The 40mg dose was effective only for vomiting. For every other outcome, 80mg was needed.

Who benefits most from NK1 antagonists?
→Women — vomiting Risk Ratio 0.41, statistically significant benefit
→Laparoscopic and gynaecological surgery — highest efficacy shown
→Patients receiving inhalational anaesthesia — maintains efficacy despite volatile agents
→Apfel score 3–4 — maximum benefit in the highest-risk population
⚠️
NK1 antagonists are metabolised by CYP3A4. Theoretical interactions exist with CYP3A4 inhibitors, inducers, and oral contraceptives — but clinical significance is minimal for single-dose perioperative use. No dose adjustment is needed in most cases.

The Clinical Strategy

Three pillars · Risk-stratified · Evidence-based

Now we put it all together. The strategy for PONV prevention is built on three pillars — and the first pillar has nothing to do with antiemetics at all.

Pillar 01

Baseline Risk Reduction — Before Any Drug

  • →Regional anaesthesia where feasible — reduces PONV by 25%
  • →TIVA with propofol instead of volatile agents — reduces PONV by 25%
  • →Avoid nitrous oxide — 10–15% further reduction
  • →Multimodal analgesia to minimise opioid use
  • →Adequate hydration — 20–30 ml/kg crystalloid
  • →Sugammadex over neostigmine for reversal
Pillar 02

Pharmacological Prophylaxis — Risk-Stratified

  • LowApfel 0–1: No routine prophylaxis. Focus on baseline reduction. Have rescue available.
  • ModApfel 2: Dexamethasone 8mg at induction + Ondansetron 4mg at end of surgery — OR — Aprepitant 80mg pre-operatively.
  • HighApfel 3: Triple therapy — Dexamethasone + Ondansetron + Droperidol. Or NK1-based — Aprepitant + Dexamethasone + Ondansetron.
  • VHighApfel 4: TIVA. Aprepitant 80mg or Fosaprepitant 150mg + Dexamethasone + Ondansetron + Droperidol if appropriate. Consider transdermal scopolamine.
Pillar 03

Rescue Treatment — When Prophylaxis Fails

  • →First — rule out other causes. Hypotension. Hypoxia. Pain. Surgical complications.
  • →Always use a different drug class from prophylaxis.
  • →If ondansetron given prophylactically → Metoclopramide 10mg IV or Promethazine 12.5mg IV
  • →Small-dose propofol 20mg IV — effective in the recovery room
  • →Wait 6 hours before re-dosing the same agent
The rescue principle — and why it matters

If a patient received ondansetron prophylactically and develops PONV in the recovery room — giving more ondansetron is no more effective than placebo. The receptor is already saturated. Switch class. Always.

Clinical Pearls

High-yield · Exam-ready · Practice-changing

  1. 01
    The Apfel Score is fundamental. Memorise it. Female, Non-smoker, History of PONV or motion sickness, Postoperative opioids. Four factors. Each adds ~20% to a baseline of 10%. This is the most consistently examined PONV concept.
  2. 02
    NK1 antagonists are in a different league. The only antiemetics that work as well alone as most combinations of other drugs. Fosaprepitant — 94% reduction in vomiting (RR 0.06). Aprepitant — 74% reduction (RR 0.26). Nothing else comes close.
  3. 03
    Timing matters for conventional agents. Dexamethasone at induction — it has a slow onset and long duration, covering the entire postoperative period. Ondansetron and droperidol at end of surgery — for early recovery. This timing optimisation is high-yield.
  4. 04
    Ondansetron 4mg = Ondansetron 8mg for PONV. Higher doses only increase the risk of QT prolongation. This is a common exam trap and an important clinical point. Do not reflexively double the dose.
  5. 05
    TIVA is a natural antiemetic. Propofol-based total intravenous anaesthesia reduces PONV by 25% — the same magnitude as adding a prophylactic antiemetic. Volatile anaesthetics are the most important emetogenic factor in the first two hours postoperatively.
  6. 06
    Aprepitant 80mg > 40mg. The 80mg dose reduces nausea, vomiting, AND rescue antiemetic use. The 40mg dose only reduces vomiting. For complete PONV prophylaxis — 80mg is the dose. This is the 2025 evidence.
  7. 07
    For rescue — switch class, always. Ondansetron given prophylactically is ineffective as rescue. Use a dopamine antagonist — metoclopramide or promethazine — or small-dose propofol. The mechanism must be different.

back to that patient

She had all four Apfel risk factors.

Female. Non-smoker. History of PONV from a previous procedure. And she would need opioids for postoperative pain. We had given ondansetron. We had given dexamethasone. And she still vomited — because in very high-risk patients, two agents may simply not be enough.

What the 2025 evidence tells us is that in this patient — a patient with four risk factors — the correct approach would have been to either add a third agent, use TIVA, or consider an NK1 antagonist that alone would have been as effective as our two-drug combination.

We did not fail because we gave the wrong drug. We failed because we underestimated the risk.

Risk stratification is not paperwork.
It is the difference between a patient who recovers comfortably
and one who spends her recovery room time vomiting.
